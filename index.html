About

Welcome to Exoplanet Search! This is a website which investigates how certain Exoplanet discovery methods are correlated with characteristics of Exoplanets (e.g. planet orbital radius around its host star).


In particular, this website focuses on four different Exoplanet discovery classes:

  • Transit
  • Radial Velocity
  • Microlensing
  • Imaging






This site not only employs machine learning to predict an Exoplanet's discovery method using select characteristics, but it also allows the user to query planets and generate a planet map. This map will then show the user all of their queried Exoplanets and their position relative to Earth!

Transit

Discovering an exoplanet using the Transit method involves measuring dips in light of a given star after one of its planets passes through it.


This is ideal for:

  • Planets whose orbits pass between Earth and its host star
  • Planets with a small orbital radius
  • Planets with high mass (e.g. gas giants)











Radial Velocity

With the Radial Velocity method, the gravitational pull of a given star's exoplanet causes the host star to "wobble." When the star is shifted towards Earth, a blueshift occurs; conversely, when the star is shifted away from Earth,a redshift occurs. It is this change of observed light that makes the discovery possible.


This is ideal for:

  • Star systems that are closer to Earth (necessary observable shift of visible light)
  • Planets that produce a significant gravitational pull on their host star
    • Closer planets with lower mass
    • Larger planets with higher mass






Microlensing

Using Microlensing involves looking at the effects that the gravitational pull that an exoplanet and its host star has on another star.

As the planet orbits its host star, their gravity can temporarily amplify the brightness of another distant star, giving the effect of a lens or magnifying glass.


This is ideal for:

  • Star systems that are distant from Earth
  • Planets with a wider orbital radius







Imaging

The Imaging method involves taking an actual picture of the exoplanet.


This is best suited for:

  • Star systems that are closer to Earth
  • Planets with a large mass
  • Planets with a wider orbital radius
